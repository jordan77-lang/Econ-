▸ Objective 1 · Steps 4 through 6 — Average Cost, Marginal Cost, and Cost Patterns

⬡ Objective 1 · Steps 4 through 6 — Average Cost, Marginal Cost, and Cost Patterns

What You're Doing

This section covers mission memo Steps 4 through 6 in one terminal workspace: calculate measures of average cost (AFC, AVC, ATC), calculate marginal cost (MC), then use Chart A to interpret cost patterns across output levels. Round all answers to 3 decimal places.

The Four Formulas

AFC = FC ÷ Q   (Average Fixed Cost per bottle)
AVC = VC ÷ Q   (Average Variable Cost per bottle)
ATC = TC ÷ Q   (Average Total Cost per bottle)
MC = ΔTC ÷ ΔQ (Marginal Cost — change in TC ÷ change in Q)

Curve Behavior (Firm Theory)

  • AFC always falls as output rises — the same fixed overhead is spread across more bottles.
  • AVC is typically U-shaped: average variable cost falls at low output, then rises as capacity strains.
  • ATC is U-shaped because AFC pulls it down at low Q while rising AVC pulls it up at high Q. ATC is minimized where MC = ATC.
  • MC intersects AVC and ATC at their minimum points — a standard result in cost theory.

Marginal Cost (MC) — Special Rule

  • MC measures the extra cost of producing the next batch of bottles — calculated between two output levels, not at a single Q.
  • Formula: MC = (TC at upper Q − TC at lower Q) ÷ (upper Q − lower Q)
  • After each output row, enter MC in the MC column on the line to the next Q — each cell is marked with › directly to the left of the input.
  • Each interval spans 240 bottles. The first interval is 960 through 1,200; MC there ($0.219) is slightly below MR ($0.25) because profit is still rising toward the peak.
  • Because each MC value is calculated across an interval (not at a single Q), the highlighted MC point for that interval is plotted at the midpoint between the two outputs (for example, 1,680 through 1,920 is plotted at Q = 1,800).
  • Charts plot a smooth MC curve from the same cost equation — it sweeps upward at low Q and crosses MR at Q = 1,200. The highlighted midpoint dots mark your four calculated MC interval values. Chart C shows where AVC crosses the $0.25 price line at high output (short-run shutdown).

How to Enter Answers

  • Fill in MC in the MC column between rows (on the › line, not on the Q row itself).

▸ Objective 2 · Step 2 — Calculate Profit at Different Outputs

⬡ Objective 2 · Step 2 — Calculate Profit at Different Outputs

What You're Doing

Revenue indicates the amount of money Parfum du coeur generates from sales, but profit is the true measure of its financial performance. Profit is the difference between what the firm earns and what it spends to operate. A firm can appear successful if bottles are selling quickly, but if costs outweigh revenue, the business will ultimately fail to survive.

In this step, you will combine the cost data you have already calculated with the market price of perfume to estimate profit at different output levels. This analysis will show how profit changes as output increases, providing a clearer picture of Parfum du coeur's overall performance.

The Formulas

Market Price = $0.25 per bottle (perfect competition → P = MR)
Total Revenue (TR) = Price × Output = $0.25 × Q
Profit (π) = Total Revenue − Total Cost = TR − TC

Using the Total Cost (TC) values you completed in Objective 1 (already shown in the table), continue these calculations for every output until the table is complete.

Firm Decisions (Preview — Objective 3 in Canvas)

  • Profit maximization: produce where MC = MR (see Chart B). Your profit table should peak near that output.
  • Short-run shutdown: if Price < AVC, the firm shuts down temporarily to minimize loss (Chart C).
  • Long-run exit: if Price < ATC, the firm cannot cover all costs and should leave the industry in the long run (Chart C).
  • At a given output level, always compare price to AVC for the short-run decision and to ATC for the long-run decision.
