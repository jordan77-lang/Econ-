▸ OBJECTIVE 1 · STEP 1 — FIXED vs VARIABLE COST CLASSIFICATION
⬡ STEP 1 INSTRUCTIONS — FIXED vs VARIABLE COST CLASSIFICATION

What You're Doing

Wheeler & Pace's monthly expense ledger lists 9 expenses. Your job is to sort each one into the correct cost category before you can calculate totals in Step 2.

The Two Categories

  • Fixed Costs — expenses that stay the same every month no matter how many bottles of perfume are produced. Even if output drops to zero, these costs are still owed.
  • Variable Costs — expenses that change based on how much is produced. More bottles means more of these inputs are needed and purchased.

The Key Test

For each expense, ask yourself: "If the perfumery produced zero bottles this month, would this cost still appear on the bill?"

▸ YES — still owed at zero output → Fixed Cost
▸ NO — disappears when nothing is produced → Variable Cost

How to Classify

  • The 9 expense cards are shown in a shuffled pool at the top.
  • Drag each card from the pool and drop it into either the Fixed Costs or Variable Costs bin below.
  • On a tablet, press and hold a card, then drag it to the correct bin.
  • Once all 9 cards are placed, a VERIFY CLASSIFICATION button will appear.
  • Click VERIFY — correct cards turn green and stay. Wrong cards turn red and return to the pool so you can try again.

Scoring

  • You can verify as many times as needed — there is no penalty for wrong attempts.
  • Once all 9 are correctly classified, Step 2 will unlock automatically.
⬡ N.I.B.B.L.E.S. tip: Think about whether the expense is a contract or commitment (fixed) versus a consumable input that gets used up during production (variable). Contracts don't care how busy you are — raw materials do.

▸ OBJECTIVE 1 · STEP 4 — AVERAGE & MARGINAL COSTS
⬡ STEP 4 INSTRUCTIONS — AVERAGE & MARGINAL COSTS

What You're Doing

For each output level, calculate four per-unit cost metrics using the FC, VC, and TC values from Step 3. Round all answers to 3 decimal places.

The Four Formulas

AFC = FC ÷ Q   (Average Fixed Cost per bottle)
AVC = VC ÷ Q   (Average Variable Cost per bottle)
ATC = TC ÷ Q   (Average Total Cost per bottle)
MC = ΔTC ÷ ΔQ (Marginal Cost — change in TC ÷ change in Q)

Marginal Cost (MC) — Special Rule

  • MC measures the extra cost of producing the next batch of bottles.
  • Formula: MC = (TC at this row − TC at previous row) ÷ (Q at this row − Q at previous row)
  • The first row (Q = 720) has no previous row, so MC is N/A — that cell is already disabled.
  • Every other row has a MC value to calculate.

How to Enter Answers

  • Fill in all four cells for a row and press the ▶ CHECK button (or Enter on any cell) to verify the whole row at once.
  • All cells in a row must be correct before that row turns green.
  • All 11 rows verified → the cost curve chart unlocks and the Proceed button appears.
⬡ N.I.B.B.L.E.S. tip: Notice that ATC reaches its minimum around Q = 1,080. That is not a coincidence — it is where the firm is most efficient. Watch the chart fill in as you verify each row.

▸ OBJECTIVE 2 · STEP 1 — MARGINAL REVENUE (MR)
⬡ OBJECTIVE 2 · STEP 1 BRIEFING — MARGINAL REVENUE

What You're Doing

Wheeler & Pace operates in a perfectly competitive market. Your job is to find and enter the market price per bottle — this is also the firm's Marginal Revenue (MR).

Key Concept

  • In a perfectly competitive market, the firm is a price taker — it cannot set its own price.
  • Every bottle sells for the same market price, no matter how many bottles are produced.
  • Because price is constant, MR = Market Price at every output level.

Where to Find It

The market price per bottle is stated directly in the Objective 2 introduction section of your assignment. Look for a dollar-and-cents value between $0.20 and $0.30 per bottle.

⬡ OBJECTIVE 2 · STEP 2 BRIEFING — TOTAL REVENUE & PROFIT

What You're Doing

Revenue indicates the amount of money the perfumery generates from sales, but profit is the true measure of its financial performance. Profit is the difference between what the firm earns and what it spends to operate. A firm can appear successful if bottles are selling quickly, but if costs outweigh revenue, the business will ultimately fail to survive.

In this step, you will combine the cost data you have already calculated with the market price of perfume to estimate profit at different output levels. This analysis will show how profit changes as output increases, providing a clearer picture of the perfumery’s overall performance.

The Formulas

Market Price = $0.25 per bottle
Total Revenue (TR) = $0.25 × Output
Profit = Total Revenue (TR) − Total Cost (TC)

Using the Total Cost (TC) values you completed in Objective 1 (already shown in the table), continue these calculations for every output until the table is complete.

How to Enter Answers

  • In the YOUR TR column, calculate revenue for each output and press Enter.
  • Next, move to the YOUR PROFIT column and calculate profit for each output and press Enter.
